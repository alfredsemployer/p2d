smil on energy and the AI scaling question

2026‑05‑18 · ~6 min

The training-compute story is told almost entirely in FLOPs. Vaclav Smil would say that's the story you tell when you don't want to look at the bill.

A single H100 draws roughly 700 watts under load. A B200, closer to 1.2 kilowatts. A 100,000-GPU cluster, running flat-out, is somewhere between 70 and 150 megawatts continuous — without counting cooling, which typically adds another 30–50%. That is the load of a small city, and we are now building several of them per year.

the units that matter

When you read that “OpenAI trained GPT-X for 1026 FLOPs,” the more useful translation is: somewhere between three and six terawatt-hours of electricity, plus the embodied energy of the chips, plus the water for cooling. Smil's whole career has been about insisting we convert clever abstractions back into joules and tons.

The most consequential numbers are usually the boring ones.

What the value-chain map I'm building tries to do is keep these numbers visible. When you click on a model lab, the side panel doesn't just show parameters and benchmark scores. It shows the upstream load — the GW-hours, the megatons of cooling water, the rare-earth supply path through three intermediaries before it lands in a GPU on a tray in a rack in a building in Loudoun County.

where this lands

The current consensus is that AI training will need ~100 GW of new generation by 2030. That number floats around without much context, so here it is in context: U.S. nuclear today is ~95 GW total. We are proposing to build, in five years, the energy equivalent of the entire U.S. nuclear fleet — for one industry, that did not exist in its current form ten years ago.

I don't think the punchline is “therefore scaling will stop.” The punchline is that whichever layer of the stack is the binding constraint — and right now it is energy, not compute — deserves a different kind of attention than it's getting.
